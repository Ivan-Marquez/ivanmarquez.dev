A bit more about me

What have you learn the most in your career?

When it comes to Software Development, you have to learn how to be comfortable working in the edge of not knowing. I've learned to be confident even when I don't know how to solve a problem because it is impossible to know everything in this line of work. When you're confident, you have no problem to admit you don't know how to solve something and that you need help from others, or that you need more time to do research. It's better than just trying to fake it or trying to be a superhero.

I've learned that one of the most important things in software development is to fully understand the problem to be solved before trying to solve it. Sure, you need to keep yourself up to date with the latest technologies and programming languages. But thinking from the user standpoint and taking enough time to understand what needs to be done is crucial.

"If I had twenty days to solve a problem, I would take nineteen to define it." - Albert Einstein

What’s the single most important thing you learned in the last year and how has it changed the way you work today?

User standpoint. I've learned to see the problems from the user's point of view, which allows me to design workflows and solutions that completely meet the user's requirements. It doesn't matter how well-crafted a product may be. If it doesn't solve the user's problem, it cannot be considered of high quality.

What achievement are you most proud of?

I recently contributed to the design of an ElasticSearch solution to increase query response time. The search part of the website was too slow (millions of records in the database) and I came up with a loosely coupled and fault-tolerant solution to integrate and sync data between MongoDB and ElasticSearch. I took full ownership of the architecture design and implementation as well as the creation of development tasks for our team.

As a result, the response time came from 9 seconds down to 200 - 300 milliseconds. And it was fun!
